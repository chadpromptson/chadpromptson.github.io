A beer and an architecture decision

Yesterday, Uncle Martin came over for a beer with Dad. Chad showed him PromptLooper and the diagram for a factory whose five products would eventually find ideas, write prompts, build companies, improve them, and launch everything overnight.

“You need a monorepo. All five parts of the factory need to work together.”

— Uncle Martin, before the repository contained five parts

Chad looked it up. Large technology companies use monorepos. That was enough to approve the architecture decision. The only remaining question was what to put in the initial commit.

Initial commit rationale

Never change a winning team.

PromptLooper was finished, tested twice, and proven in production. Replacing any part of it would introduce unnecessary risk. Chad therefore copied the complete repository into the new enterprise monorepo.

GenX.AI, MetaPrompt.AI, RecLoop.AI, and SleepShip.AI were pre-integrated conceptually. Their folders would be added when required, avoiding premature files.

Chad

“The AI built an overengineered backend while I was sleeping.”

Uncle Martin

“What makes it overengineered?”

Chad

Uncle Martin

The line counter had no field for that question. Chad had optimized the rule, but the rule could not tell him whether GenX found a useful opportunity, preserved its source, or could be understood tomorrow. For the first time, the founder kept a human in the loop on purpose.
